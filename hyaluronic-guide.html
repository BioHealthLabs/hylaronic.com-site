Hyaluronic Acid Guide: How to Get Plump, Hydrated Skin Without the Sticky Mess

Hyaluronic acid (HA) is one of the most hyped ingredients in skincare – and for once, the hype is mostly deserved. Used correctly, it can make your skin look smoother, bouncier and more hydrated. Used badly, it can actually make dryness worse.

What is hyaluronic acid?

Hyaluronic acid is a molecule that naturally exists in your skin, eyes and joints. It can bind up to 1,000 times its own weight in water and plays a key role in keeping your skin plump and cushioned.

In skincare, “hyaluronic acid” usually refers to different forms of HA used in serums, essences and creams to increase surface hydration and improve skin feel.

What does hyaluronic acid actually do?

  • Boosts surface hydration: helps skin look smoother and more dewy.
  • Softens fine lines (temporarily): by plumping up the top layers of the skin.
  • Improves product feel: gives serums that “bouncy”, gel-like texture.
  • Supports barrier function: hydrated skin usually tolerates actives better.

Who should use hyaluronic acid?

Almost everyone can benefit from well-formulated hyaluronic products:

  • Dry or dehydrated skin: extra hydration between cleanser and moisturiser.
  • Oily skin: lightweight hydration without heavy creams.
  • Retinol and acid users: helps offset some of the dryness from actives.
  • Air-conditioned or heated environments: where skin loses water faster.

How to use hyaluronic acid correctly

This is where most people go wrong. Hyaluronic acid serums are not meant to replace your moisturiser or sunscreen – they sit in between.

  1. Cleanse with a gentle, non-stripping cleanser.
  2. On slightly damp skin, apply a few drops of your hyaluronic serum.
  3. Follow with a moisturiser to “seal in” the hydration.
  4. Use sunscreen (SPF 30+) every morning as usual.

If you apply hyaluronic acid on bone-dry skin and skip moisturiser on top, it can feel tight and paradoxically more dry.

Common forms of hyaluronic acid

When you read the ingredient list, you might see different names:

  • Sodium hyaluronate: the most common, salt form of hyaluronic acid.
  • Hydrolysed hyaluronic acid: smaller fragments designed to penetrate a bit deeper.
  • Sodium acetylated hyaluronate: modified form claimed to cling better to the skin.

Many modern serums combine several molecular weights to target different layers of the skin surface – more on that in our advanced hyaluronic guide.

How often can you use hyaluronic acid?

Unlike strong actives such as retinoids or exfoliating acids, hyaluronic acid is typically safe to use once or twice daily for most people.

If a product feels sticky, pills under makeup or causes breakouts, the issue is usually the overall formula (thickeners, film-formers, fragrance) – not the hyaluronic acid itself.

Biggest mistakes people make with hyaluronic acid

  • Using it without moisturiser: HA needs something occlusive on top.
  • Applying way too much: a thin layer is enough – more just feels tacky.
  • Layering 3–4 HA products: redundant and can lead to sticky buildup.
  • Expecting it to “fix” wrinkles: it hydrates, it doesn’t replace retinoids or SPF.

Can you combine hyaluronic acid with other actives?

Yes. In fact, hyaluronic acid plays well with almost everything:

  • With vitamin C: HA after your vitamin C serum can help reduce dryness.
  • With retinol: supports hydration in a routine that might otherwise be drying.
  • With niacinamide: soothing, barrier-friendly combination.

The key is still to avoid overloading your routine. A simple routine used consistently beats a 12-step routine used twice a month.

How to choose a good hyaluronic serum

  • Look for fragrance-free formulas if you have sensitive skin.
  • Prioritise serums that combine HA with other hydrating ingredients (glycerin, urea, panthenol).
  • Avoid very heavy, sticky gels if you’re oily and acne-prone.
  • Don’t overpay just for “fancy water” – mid-priced serums can work extremely well.
